What is Oral Cancer?

Oral cancer, also known as mouth cancer or oral cavity cancer, refers to cancer that develops in any part of the mouth, including the lips, tongue, gums, floor of the mouth, roof of the mouth (hard and soft palate), inner lining of the cheeks, and the area behind the wisdom teeth.

Most oral cancers are squamous cell carcinomas, which begin in the flat, thin cells that line the mouth and throat. Early detection is crucial as it significantly improves treatment outcomes and survival rates.

Warning Signs:

  • Persistent mouth sores that don't heal
  • White or red patches in the mouth
  • Lumps or thickening of tissues
  • Difficulty swallowing or chewing
  • Persistent sore throat or hoarseness
  • Numbness in the mouth or lips

Risk Factors

Tobacco Use

Cigarettes, cigars, pipes, chewing tobacco, and snuff significantly increase risk

Alcohol Consumption

Heavy drinking increases risk, especially when combined with tobacco

HPV Infection

Human papillomavirus, particularly HPV-16, is linked to oral cancers

Sun Exposure

Prolonged sun exposure increases risk of lip cancer

Other Factors

Age (over 40), gender (more common in men), poor nutrition, weakened immune system

Types of Oral Cancer

Understanding the different types of oral cancer helps in early detection and appropriate treatment

Squamous Cell Carcinoma

The most common type, accounting for over 90% of oral cancers. It develops in the squamous cells that line the mouth and throat.

Characteristics:

  • Can appear as white or red patches (leukoplakia or erythroplakia)
  • May present as ulcers that don't heal
  • Often rough or crusty in texture
  • Can develop anywhere in the oral cavity

Reference: Neville, B. W., & Day, T. A. (2002). Oral cancer and precancerous lesions. CA: A Cancer Journal for Clinicians, 52(4), 195-215.

Verrucous Carcinoma

A rare, slow-growing variant of squamous cell carcinoma that rarely metastasizes. It has a warty, cauliflower-like appearance.

Characteristics:

  • Appears as a white, thick, raised lesion
  • Warty or cauliflower-like surface texture
  • Slow-growing and locally invasive
  • Common sites: buccal mucosa, gingiva, tongue
  • Associated with tobacco use, especially smokeless tobacco

Reference: Koch, B. B., et al. (2001). National survey of head and neck verrucous carcinoma. Cancer, 92(1), 110-120.

Minor Salivary Gland Carcinomas

A group of cancers that develop in the minor salivary glands scattered throughout the mouth, particularly in the palate, tongue, and lips.

Common Types Include:

  • Mucoepidermoid carcinoma - Most common type
  • Adenoid cystic carcinoma - Slow-growing but aggressive
  • Polymorphous adenocarcinoma - Usually in the palate
  • Acinic cell carcinoma - Rare in minor glands

Reference: Barnes, L., et al. (2005). Pathology and Genetics of Head and Neck Tumours. WHO Classification of Tumours, IARC Press.

Oral Lymphomas

Cancers of the lymphatic tissue in the oral cavity, most commonly Non-Hodgkin's lymphoma. These can develop in the tonsils, base of tongue, or other lymphoid tissues.

Characteristics:

  • May present as painless swelling or mass
  • Can affect tonsils, palate, or gingiva
  • Often appears as a submucosal swelling
  • May be associated with systemic symptoms
  • More common in immunocompromised patients

Reference: Kemp, S., et al. (2008). Oral non-Hodgkin's lymphoma: review of the literature and World Health Organization classification. Oral Surgery, Oral Medicine, Oral Pathology, 105(2), 194-201.

Oral Malignant Melanoma

A rare but aggressive form of oral cancer arising from melanocytes. It accounts for less than 2% of all oral malignancies but has a poor prognosis.

Characteristics:

  • Appears as pigmented (brown, black, blue) or non-pigmented lesion
  • Most common sites: hard palate and maxillary gingiva
  • Can be flat or raised, irregular borders
  • May bleed easily or ulcerate
  • Highly aggressive with tendency to metastasize early

Reference: Hicks, M. J., & Flaitz, C. M. (2000). Oral mucosal melanoma: epidemiology and pathobiology. Oral Oncology, 36(2), 152-169.

Kaposi's Sarcoma

A vascular tumor caused by Human Herpesvirus 8 (HHV-8), commonly seen in immunocompromised individuals, particularly those with HIV/AIDS.

Characteristics:

  • Appears as purple, red, or brown patches or nodules
  • Common sites: palate, gingiva, tongue
  • Starts as flat lesions, may become raised
  • Can be multifocal (multiple sites)
  • Strongly associated with immunosuppression

Reference: Antman, K., & Chang, Y. (2000). Kaposi's sarcoma. New England Journal of Medicine, 342(14), 1027-1038.

Prevention & Early Detection

Most oral cancers are preventable through lifestyle changes and regular screening

Avoid Tobacco

Quit smoking and avoid all tobacco products including cigarettes, cigars, pipes, and smokeless tobacco. Tobacco use is the single largest risk factor for oral cancer.

Limit Alcohol

Reduce alcohol consumption. Heavy drinking, especially when combined with tobacco use, significantly increases the risk of oral cancer.

Healthy Diet

Eat a balanced diet rich in fruits and vegetables. Proper nutrition strengthens the immune system and may help reduce cancer risk.

Sun Protection

Use lip balm with SPF and limit sun exposure to reduce the risk of lip cancer. Reapply regularly when outdoors.

HPV Vaccination

Get vaccinated against HPV, which can cause certain oral cancers. The vaccine is most effective when given before exposure.

Regular Screenings

Visit your dentist regularly for oral cancer screenings. Early detection dramatically improves treatment success rates.
